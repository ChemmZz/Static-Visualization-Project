Made in China: Installed everywhere

China is the central node in the global energy transition

China is a solar titan, it supplies the majority of the world's solar manufacturing inputs and finished modules they manufacture over 80% of all the solar panels stages and hosts 9 of the Top 10 PV manufacturers (https://www.woodmac.com/press-releases/global-solar-ranking/).

Photovoltaic module distribution worldwide (2023). Source: Statista https://www.statista.com/statistics/668749/regional-distribution-of-solar-pv-module-manufacturing/

When talking about renewables it's important to understand that countries are not buying an energy source, unlike oil that will run out in a relatively small time, once you buy a solar panel it's good for the next 25-35 years, a country is buying not a energy source, rather it's buying a technology that empowers it and makes it less dependent on others, less exposed to global conflicts, i.e. makes energy sovereignty a possiblity, descentrilization. A such, solar beign a technology not a fuel it can power whatever the country needs, lights, factories, heaters, farms. And as Gabriel Davies has explained, (https://energyaccess.duke.edu/the-secret-of-the-energy-transition-why-solar-will-reshape-global-politics-and-be-bad-for-putin/) solar practicallt achieves the same efficiency in small as in giant solar farms, meaning small-scale local solar can compite with the more traditional centralized lare-scale power generation

Thus as China's solar technology exports continue to grow, it's important to understand where are they going and how the countries importing them are using the technology, is it to become less reliable in other conuntries, to achieve internal carbon footprint policies or maybe to compete against China. In this post I will deep dive into where the money from solar is going, what are the big importers doing with it and where is China directly investing in solar farms

Inside the supply chain

Key stages in the main manufacturing process for solar PV. Source: International Energy Agency https://www.iea.org/reports/solar-pv-global-supply-chains/executive-summary

Since 2022 the General Administration of Customs of the People's Republic of China (GACC) and the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) started reporting panel and cell exports separately cell and wafers exports have been wining traction and now summed both they make up more than 40% of China's solar technology exports

Two types of importers

Some countries rely on China to power solar deployment, while others rely on China to power their own manufacturing capabillity

Depending on the partner China enables both deployment and industrial capability. From the Top 5 importers of Chinese energy technology, 3 (India, Vietnam, Thailand) are direct competitors in the manufacturing scene and consistenly trying to win more market capitalzation over China (https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-02-25/india-mulls-1-billion-subsidy-plan-to-boost-solar-manufacturing) while other countries in the Top 5 like the Netherlands or Brazil this imports come from domestic demand booming (https://apnews.com/article/brazil-wind-solar-power-electricity-renewable-energy-13eef596389803e8b801f26e7888fb97) (https://www.reuters.com/markets/commodities/netherlands-shines-key-european-energy-transition-driver-2024-03-05/).

We can see this clearly in the graph by looking at those countries that only import panels vs those who are importing the intermediate techonologies. All of these are South East Asian, marking a trend on what could be the future of the technology and economics of the region.

When China Doesn't Just Exports: It invests

China is also creating its own market

Unlike raw exports, China's direct investments tends to target countries. where: - Energy transition is accelerating fast (UAE, Chile) - Local capital markets are tight (Argentina) - Grid expansion plans depend on foreign financing (Italy)
